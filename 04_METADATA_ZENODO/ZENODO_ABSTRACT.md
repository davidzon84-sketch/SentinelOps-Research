## Abstract (Deposit Text)

Modern organizations operate across complex software supply chains in which the transition from design to execution or external delivery is often treated as progress by default. When security assumptions degrade, review scope remains incomplete, or intellectual-property boundaries prohibit conventional code sharing, the imperative to build, package, and ship can increase exposure rather than reduce it. This deposit presents the **Controlled Evaluation Boundary (CEB)**, a governance-first conceptual model for external research review under explicit boundary separation.

CEB reframes execution control as a first-class systems problem: the decision to assemble, compile, or deliver is a security-sensitive state transition that demands documented authorization, separable integrity and provenance evidence, and accountable human judgmentâ€”not implicit velocity. A central contribution is the formal recognition that **non-execution**â€”the deliberate withholding of build or delivery when preconditions are unmetâ€”is a valid and often preferred security outcome, not a program failure.

The artifact documents authorization states, open-question gates, fail-closed transition semantics, and audit-evidence templates at the research level. It does not disclose product implementation, certify compliance, or constitute deployable software. The model offers systems researchers, cloud security architects, and program reviewers a reproducible pattern for governing when shared artifacts may become validly executable or deliverable to external evaluators under adversarial and supply-chain uncertainty.
